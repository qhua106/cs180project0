Selfie: The Wrong Way vs. The Right Way

The "Wrong" Way: Close-up

The "Right" Way: Stepped Back & Zoomed In

Why The Difference?

The first image, taken close-up with a wide-angle lens, suffers from perspective distortion. Features closer to the camera (like the nose) appear disproportionately larger than those further away.

In the second photo, by increasing the distance to the subject and using optical zoom, the camera captures a narrower field of view, and the relative distances between facial features become much smaller compared to the overall camera-to-subject distance. This compresses the features, resulting in a more natural portrait that better reflects how we see people in real life.

Part 2

Architectural Perspective Compression

Telephoto Shot: Far Away & Zoomed In

Wide Shot: Close Up & No Zoom

Flattening Space

The first image demonstrates perspective compression. By standing far away and zooming in, the distance between foreground objects and background objects appears significantly reduced. This creates a "flattened" effect where layers of the scene seem stacked on top of one another.

The second, wide-angle shot provides a more familiar sense of depth and scale. The perspective lines converge more dramatically, and objects in the foreground appear much larger than those in the distance, giving a stronger illusion of three-dimensional space.

Part 3

The Dolly Zoom (Vertigo Effect)

How It Works

The Dolly Zoom, famously known as the "Vertigo shot," is a powerful cinematic effect. It is achieved by combining two opposite camera actions simultaneously: physically moving the camera (dollying) towards or away from a subject while simultaneously zooming the lens in the opposite direction.

The result is that the main subject in the foreground stays roughly the same size and position in the frame, while the background appears to warp‚Äîeither expanding dramatically or compressing inwards.
